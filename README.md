**Demo**
This demo isn't a dashboard; it’s a Causal Graph Simulation. The entire premise of the code is built on one financial reality: ERPs lie. Accounting software looks at a snapshot in time (P&L, Balance Sheet), but liquidity is a continuous flow dictated by operational friction.

Here is the financial breakdown of the scenarios this code actually models, tab by tab, and how they mathematically destroy or create value.
**Tab 1: The Causal Cascade (The Liquidity Squeeze)**
The Financial Scenario: The "Silent Default." Your ERP reports $8.0M in cash and a healthy profit. However, your operations are drifting. Customers are paying later, suppliers want cash faster, and inventory is sitting longer.
A 10-day delay in customer payments (DSO) doesn't just mean cash is late. The code mathematically proves that this delay drains your working capital until you breach your bank's DSCR covenant floor of 1.20x.
The Impact: The moment that DSCR drops below 1.20, the code triggers accelerationRisk. This is the nightmare scenario where a bank legally demands immediate repayment of a $4.2M facility. 

**Takeaway**: Operational friction causes legal bankruptcy, even if the P&L says you are profitable.

**Tab 2: Dead Money Recovery (The Asset Illusion)**
The Financial Scenario: Extracting zero-cost capital from a bloated balance sheet. The ERP claims you have $8.4M in inventory. The CFO knows half of that is a lie—it's dead weight that will never convert to cash at book value.
What it Actually Means: The code is modeling an "opportunity cost" audit. It’s showing that holding $1.2M of dead inventory at "book value" to make the balance sheet look pretty is financially toxic. Liquidating it for $900K cash is mathematically superior because it provides the liquidity needed to survive the covenant stress tested in Tab 1.

**The Impact:** It also audits "Vendor credit delta." The code flags that paying suppliers 18 days before the contract requires it is a massive, invisible leak of zero-cost financing.

**Tab 3: Whale Curve (Capital Destroyers)**
The Financial Scenario: Firing your biggest customers. Accounting says every customer generates a 25% gross margin. The Whale Curve proves that your bottom 10% of customers are actually being subsidized by the top 10%.
What it Actually Means: To calculate "Net economic yield," the code subtracts the cost of capital (capital drag) and dispute friction from the reported 25% margin. Because Customer Sigma takes 110 days to pay, the cost to finance their specific receivables completely wipes out their margin, resulting in a -56.4% true yield.

**The Impact:** Those customers aren't just less profitable; they are actively destroying the cash generated by your good customers. The scenario forces a strategic mandate: raise prices aggressively on the bottom decile, or fire them entirely to instantly improve corporate liquidity.

**Tab 4: Maritime Intelligence (Temporal Misalignment)**
The Financial Scenario: The "Cash Flow Timing Trap." In heavy industries like maritime, standard accounting is dangerously disconnected from cash realities.

The Code Mechanics: * Dry-Dock (Shadow Debt): The updateDrydock() function continuously subtracts a "daily accrual rate" from reported profit. Every mile sailed accrues physical wear-and-tear that must be paid in 2 years. If you spend that "profit" today, you are borrowing from a future maintenance bill.

Voyage Tension: A spot voyage generates $105K in P&L profit. But the SVG chart maps the timeline. You pay the lease/bunker on Day 30, but freight (revenue) pays on Day 62.

What it Actually Means: A 32-day cash gap. You have a highly profitable contract, but your bank account will go negative on Day 31.

The Impact: You can't pay a lease with an invoice. The code proves that timing is more important than absolute profit. Furthermore, the "LC Timing Optimizer" shows that just switching from Standard Chartered (69 days to process paperwork) to HSBC (66 days) recovers $3,156 in pure capital costs per cycle simply by reducing bureaucratic drag.

**The Grand Unification (How They All Connect)**
The brilliance of this code is how the scenarios bleed into one another to form a single financial reality:

The Root Cause: You have capital destroyers (Tab 3) stretching their payments to 110 days.

The Compounding Effect: Because those customers aren't paying, you face the severe cash gap modeled in the Maritime Voyage (Tab 4).

The Domino Fall: This lack of incoming cash pushes your Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) down, triggering the Covenant Breach in the Cascade (Tab 1).

The Only Way Out: Your only survival mechanism without raising expensive equity is to ruthlessly liquidate the dormant inventory identified in Dead Money (Tab 2) and force your vendors to accept later payments.
